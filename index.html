Exam Overview

The BJCP Beer Judging (Tasting) Exam is a closed-book, proctored exam where you judge six beers in 90 minutes using standard BJCP scoresheets. Since you've already passed the online entrance exam, this is your path to Certified rank.

What You Need to Achieve

To earn Certified rank from the start, you need a combined score of 70% or higher. At this level, graders expect at least three of six beers to be accurately evaluated with scoresheets showing reasonably good completeness, descriptive ability, and feedback.

How Each Beer Is Graded (100 points per beer)

ComponentPointsWhat It Measures
Scoring Accuracy20How close your numeric score is to the consensus score
Perception20Can you detect what's actually in the beer? Flaws, characteristics, intensities.
Descriptive Ability20Can you describe what you perceive using appropriate vocabulary?
Completeness20Did you fill out every section thoroughly? No blank areas.
Feedback20Constructive suggestions for the brewer—what's good, what to improve, and how.
Key Insight

Only 20 of 100 points are about getting the number right. The other 80 points are about how well you write. You can earn Certified with good writing even if your scores are slightly off.

Score Ranges & What They Mean

RangeRankDescription
60–69RecognizedBasic ability; some significant gaps in perception or writing
70–79CertifiedAt least 3 of 6 beers well evaluated; reasonably complete, descriptive, accurate
80–89National*Strong evaluations; 4+ beers accurate; minimal errors
90–100National*Excellent across the board; 5+ beers nailed

*National rank also requires 20+ experience points and passing the written proficiency exam.

The 90-Minute Clock

Time Management

90 minutes ÷ 6 beers = 15 minutes per beer. This is tight. You need a systematic approach—spending roughly 3–4 minutes tasting and 10–11 minutes writing. Practice with a timer is essential.

The BJCP Scoresheet

The scoresheet is your exam paper. Knowing it inside and out is non-negotiable. Here's every section, what to write, and the points each section carries on the actual 50-point beer score.

What to Evaluate

Malt: Describe character (bready, biscuity, caramel, toasty, roasty, chocolate, coffee, etc.) and intensity (low, moderate, high).

Hops: Describe character (floral, citrus, piney, herbal, spicy, tropical, dank, etc.) and intensity. Note if absent when expected.

Fermentation/Yeast: Esters (fruity), phenols (spicy, smoky, clove-like), clean/neutral. Note any off-characters.

Other: Note anything else—alcohol warmth, DMS, diacetyl, oxidation, infection, adjuncts, spices, fruit, barrel character.

Writing Template

"[Intensity] [malt character] malt aroma with [intensity] [hop character] hop aroma. [Yeast character] is [intensity]. [Any off-flavors or additional notes]. No [expected characteristic if absent]. Overall aroma is [clean/complex/simple]."

Common Mistakes

  • Leaving hops blank instead of writing "No hop aroma detected"
  • Forgetting to comment on yeast character
  • Not noting intensity levels—just saying "malty" instead of "moderate caramel malt"

What to Evaluate

Color: Use SRM descriptors (straw, gold, amber, copper, brown, dark brown, black) and note specific shade.

Clarity: Brilliant, clear, slight haze, hazy, opaque. Note if appropriate or not for style.

Head: Color (white, off-white, tan, brown), size (none, small, moderate, large), retention (poor, moderate, good, excellent), texture (thin, creamy, rocky, mousse-like).

Carbonation: Visible carbonation level from bubble streams.

Writing Template

"Pours [color], [clarity]. [Size] [color] head with [retention] retention and [texture] texture. [Carbonation observation]. [Lacing if notable]."

Tip

Appearance is only 3 points on the scoresheet but it's easy completeness points on your exam evaluation. Never skip it or write just one word.

What to Evaluate

Malt flavor: Same descriptors as aroma, but as tasted. Note sweetness level (dry, off-dry, medium, sweet).

Hop flavor: Character and intensity, distinct from bitterness.

Hop bitterness: Low, moderate, high, assertive. How does it relate to malt? Balanced, malt-forward, hop-forward?

Fermentation character: Ester and phenol flavors, alcohol, any yeast-derived qualities.

Balance: How do the elements work together? This is critical to demonstrate.

Finish: Dry, semi-dry, medium, sweet? Clean, lingering bitterness, malty aftertaste?

Off-flavors: Identify any and note their intensity.

Writing Template

"[Intensity] [malt character] malt flavor with [sweetness level]. [Intensity] [hop character] hop flavor. Bitterness is [level], providing a [balance description]. [Fermentation character]. Finish is [description]. [Any off-flavors]. [Balance statement]."

Critical for Certified Level

You must address all of the above sub-components. Leaving out balance or finish will cost you completeness AND feedback points. Write at least 4–5 sentences here.

What to Evaluate

Body: Light, medium-light, medium, medium-full, full.

Carbonation: Low, moderate, high, prickly, spritzy, champagne-like.

Warmth/Alcohol: Note presence and whether appropriate for style and stated ABV.

Creaminess/Astringency: Silky, creamy, oily, thin, astringent, tannic.

Other: Prickle from spices, acidity/tartness as mouthfeel, residual sweetness as body.

Writing Template

"[Body level] body with [carbonation level] carbonation. [Texture descriptors]. [Alcohol presence]. [Any astringency or other tactile sensations]. Appropriate/inappropriate for style."

Common Pitfall

Astringency goes in Mouthfeel (it's a tactile sensation), not Flavor. Sourness/tartness is a flavor, but the puckering sensation can be noted in Mouthfeel as well. Putting things in the wrong section costs perception points.

What to Include

Style accuracy: Does the beer fit the declared style? What's on target, what's off?

Strengths: What does the brewer do well?

Weaknesses: What could be improved?

Actionable suggestions: How can the brewer fix issues? This is where feedback points live.

Enjoyability: A personal note on overall drinkability and pleasure.

Writing Template

"This is a [quality assessment] example of [style]. [Strength statement]. [Weakness statement]. To improve, [specific actionable brewing suggestion]. [Enjoyability comment]."

Certified-Level Expectation

Always provide at least one specific, actionable brewing suggestion—e.g., "Lower mash temperature to 148°F to achieve a drier finish" rather than just "too sweet." This is the difference between Recognized and Certified feedback.

Perception & Sensory Skills

Perception is worth 20 points per beer on the exam. It measures whether you can actually detect what's in the beer. This is a skill that must be trained through deliberate tasting practice.

The Sensory Evaluation Process

Systematic Approach

Always evaluate in order: Appearance → Aroma → Flavor → Mouthfeel. Start with appearance while the head is fresh. Take several short sniffs for aroma (don't plunge your nose in and sniff once). Take a moderate sip, let it coat your palate, then swallow and note the finish.

Short sniffs: Take 2–3 quick, short sniffs rather than one long inhale. Your olfactory receptors fatigue in about 3 seconds.

Cover and swirl: Cover the glass with your hand, swirl gently, then sniff. This concentrates volatile aromatics.

Retronasal: After sipping, exhale through your nose with your mouth closed. This catches aromas you miss by sniffing alone.

Rest between beers: Sniff the back of your hand or your sleeve to "reset" between samples. During the exam, move to the next beer if you feel fatigued.

Practice at home: Smell common ingredients—fresh hops, crushed grain, fruit—to build your reference library. Spice racks are excellent training tools.

The old "tongue map" is mostly debunked—you can detect all tastes across most of your tongue. However, some practical notes:

Sweetness: Perceived across the palate; note initial impression vs. finish.

Bitterness: Often most apparent at the back of the tongue and in the aftertaste. It accumulates with each sip.

Sourness/Acidity: Causes salivation; felt on the sides of the tongue.

Astringency: A drying, puckering sensation in the mouth (tactile, not taste—goes in Mouthfeel).

Carbonation bite: The "sting" from CO2; distinct from hop bitterness.

Intensity Descriptors (Use These Consistently)

None → Faint/Slight → Low → Moderate-Low → Moderate → Moderate-High → High → Very High

Exam Tip

Always pair a characteristic with an intensity. "Caramel malt" is vague; "moderate caramel malt" tells the grader you calibrated what you're perceiving.

Scoring accuracy (20 pts per beer) requires you to assign numeric scores that align with consensus. Here's how to calibrate:

Anchor to 30: An average homebrew competition entry with no major flaws scores around 30. Start there and adjust.

Deduct for flaws: Each noticeable flaw (diacetyl, oxidation, off-target style) might drop the score 2–5 points depending on severity.

Add for excellence: Beers that nail the style with complexity and balance climb above 35.

Use the full range: Don't cluster everything between 28–34. If a beer is flawed, score it in the teens or twenties. If it's great, go above 38.

Common Error

Palate fatigue during the exam causes scores to compress toward the middle as you progress. Consciously recalibrate after beer 3 or 4. Take a sip of water and a deep breath.

Off-Flavors & Faults

Detecting and correctly identifying off-flavors is core to the perception score. You need to know what causes them and suggest remedies (for the feedback score). These are the most commonly encountered faults.

Perception: Artificial butter, butterscotch, or toffee aroma/flavor. At higher levels, a slippery or oily mouthfeel.

Detection Tip: Warm the sample slightly in your hands; diacetyl becomes more apparent as the beer warms. Also check the finish—it often lingers as a buttery coating.

Causes: Premature separation from yeast, insufficient diacetyl rest, bacterial contamination (Pediococcus), unhealthy or insufficient yeast.

Brewing Fix: "Perform a diacetyl rest at 65–68°F for 2–3 days near the end of primary fermentation before crashing or packaging."

Acceptable in: Low levels in some English ales, Czech lagers (very low), and certain Scottish ales.

Perception: Wet cardboard, papery, stale bread at lower levels. Can progress to sherry-like, honey, or winey notes in aged/higher-ABV beers.

Detection Tip: Most apparent in the aroma. Take a big initial sniff before the beer warms; cardboard character is often the first thing to appear.

Causes: Oxygen exposure post-fermentation (hot-side oxidation during mash is debated). Poor transfers, headspace air, bad seals.

Brewing Fix: "Minimize oxygen exposure during transfers and packaging. Purge kegs/bottles with CO2 before filling. Limit headspace."

Acceptable in: Some aged strong ales, barleywines (sherry-like). Never acceptable as cardboard.

Perception: Fresh-cut green apple, sometimes cidery, green leaves, or latex paint at high levels.

Detection Tip: Most noticeable in lighter beers. Check aroma and early flavor before bitterness masks it.

Causes: Green (young) beer, premature packaging before yeast can convert acetaldehyde to ethanol. Oxidation can also reverse ethanol back to acetaldehyde.

Brewing Fix: "Allow sufficient conditioning time. Do not package too early—let primary fermentation complete fully before cold crashing or bottling."

Acceptable in: Some American light lagers at very low levels. Generally a fault.

Perception: Cooked corn, creamed corn, cooked vegetables (celery, cabbage, parsnip). At extreme levels, shellfish-like.

Detection Tip: Most noticeable in the aroma. Cover and swirl the glass, then take a quick sniff. More obvious in lighter-colored beers.

Causes: S-methyl methionine (SMM) in pale malt converted to DMS by heat. Covered boils, slow cooling, and insufficient boil vigor fail to drive it off.

Brewing Fix: "Ensure a vigorous, uncovered rolling boil for at least 60 minutes and rapidly chill wort post-boil."

Acceptable in: Very low levels in some American and light lagers.

Perception: Band-Aid, plastic, medicinal, smoky, or chlorine-like. Distinct from desirable phenols (clove, pepper) in Belgian/German wheat beers.

Detection Tip: Chlorophenols are extremely potent—detectable at parts per billion. A medicinal or plastic note in a beer that shouldn't have phenols is a clear fault.

Causes: Chlorinated water reacting with phenols, wild yeast contamination, excessive use of chlorine-based sanitizer, certain grain husks.

Brewing Fix: "Use campden tablets (potassium metabisulfite) to treat chlorinated water, or switch to reverse osmosis water. Review sanitation practices."

Acceptable in: Never (as chlorophenol). Clove/pepper phenols are expected in Weizen, Saison, Belgian styles.

Astringency

Perception: Dry, puckering, grape-skin-like sensation (mouthfeel). Like over-steeped tea.

Causes: Over-sparging, high mash pH, over-crushing grain, boiling grain, excessive hopping.

Fix: "Monitor mash pH (target 5.2–5.6). Stop sparging when runnings reach 1.010 or pH rises above 5.8."

Light-Struck (Skunky)

Perception: Skunk spray, sulfury, marijuana-like.

Causes: UV light exposure reacting with iso-alpha acids from hops. Clear and green glass bottles are most vulnerable.

Fix: "Store and package in brown bottles or cans. Avoid light exposure throughout the process."

Acetic Acid (Vinegar)

Perception: Vinegar, sharp sourness.

Causes: Acetobacter contamination (requires oxygen). Infected equipment.

Fix: "Minimize oxygen post-fermentation. Review sanitation of all equipment. Replace scratched plastic."

Fusel Alcohols (Solvent / Hot)

Perception: Harsh alcohol burn, solvent-like, warming beyond what ABV suggests.

Causes: High fermentation temperatures, under-pitching yeast, excessive simple sugars.

Fix: "Control fermentation temperature. Pitch adequate yeast. Limit adjunct sugars to under 20% of grist."

Infection (Lactic, Ropiness, Pellicle)

Perception: Sourness (lactic), ropey/viscous texture, barnyard funk.

Causes: Lactobacillus, Brettanomyces, or Pediococcus contamination. Poor sanitation.

Fix: "Replace all plastic and rubber components. Deep clean and sanitize. Consider replacing tubing and gaskets."

Style Knowledge

You won't be told the styles in advance, but you need to evaluate each beer against its declared style. Broad familiarity with the BJCP Style Guidelines is essential—you don't need to memorize every number, but you need to know what makes each style family distinctive.

What to Study

Focus on the 2021 BJCP Beer Style Guidelines. For each style you study, know: typical aroma, appearance, flavor, mouthfeel, the key differentiators from similar styles, vital statistics (OG, FG, IBU, SRM, ABV ranges), and common commercial examples.

High-Probability Exam Style Families

Exam beers are typically homebrewed and span a range of quality and styles. These families appear frequently:

Key traits: American hop character (citrus, pine, tropical, floral), clean fermentation, moderate-to-high bitterness, generally dry finish.

Differentiators: Pale Ale is moderate; IPA is hop-forward with higher bitterness/ABV; Amber shows more caramel malt balance; Brown Ale emphasizes malt with chocolate/nutty notes.

Watch for: Hop burn vs. actual bitterness, citrusy oxidation masking as hop character, overabundance of crystal malt sweetness.

Key traits: English hop character (earthy, herbal, floral), fruity esters from English yeast, malt-forward balance, moderate bitterness, lower carbonation.

Differentiators: Ordinary Bitter is session-strength and balanced; ESB is fuller and more pronounced; Porter is dark with chocolate/toffee; Stout adds roast character.

Watch for: Low diacetyl is sometimes acceptable. English yeast esters (pear, stone fruit) are expected, not faults. Don't confuse proper English malt character with sweetness flaws.

Key traits: Clean fermentation (lager yeast), noble hop character (floral, spicy, herbal), malt prominence varies by style, crisp finish.

Differentiators: Pils is hop-forward and crisp; Helles is soft and malt-accented; Dunkel is Munich malt-rich with bread/toast; Bock is strong and malty.

Watch for: DMS (common homebrew lager fault), acetaldehyde from rushed lagering, diacetyl from incomplete conditioning, sulfur (normal in young lagers, should dissipate).

Key traits: Expressive yeast character (esters and phenols), complex fermentation profiles, often higher attenuation, spicy/fruity.

Differentiators: Wit is light with coriander and orange peel, hazy; Saison is dry, peppery, fruity; Dubbel is malty, dark fruit, moderate; Tripel is strong, pale, dry, complex.

Watch for: Phenols here are EXPECTED (clove, pepper)—don't call them faults. Fusel alcohols are more common in higher-ABV Belgian styles. High attenuation means thin body is not always a flaw.

American Wheat: Clean, light, low bitterness, wheat provides haze and soft body. Not phenolic like a Hefeweizen.

Hefeweizen: Banana (isoamyl acetate) and clove (4-vinyl guaiacol) are the signature. Hazy, low bitterness, wheat tartness.

Scottish Ales: Malt-focused, very low hopping, possible light caramel/toffee, clean, low esters. NOT smoky (common misconception).

Specialty/Fruit/Spice: Must still taste like beer. The base style should be identifiable. Specialty ingredients should complement, not overwhelm.

Study Strategy

Don't try to memorize all 100+ sub-styles. Instead, learn the key parameters that differentiate similar styles (e.g., what separates a Dunkel from a Schwarzbier, or an English IPA from an American IPA). When you sit down with an exam beer, you need to evaluate it against the declared style, not recall stats from memory.

Writing & Feedback

60 of your 100 points per beer come from Descriptive Ability, Completeness, and Feedback. Strong writing can compensate for imperfect perception. Here's how to write scoresheets that score well.

Descriptive Ability (20 pts)

Use specific, sensory language. Don't say "malty"—say "moderate bready and lightly toasty malt character with a hint of caramel." Don't say "hoppy"—say "moderate-high citrus and pine hop aroma, reminiscent of Cascade or Centennial."

Malt Descriptors

Bready, biscuity, cracker-like, grainy, doughy, Pilsner-like, toasty, caramel, toffee, brown sugar, molasses, treacle, chocolate, cocoa, coffee, roasty, burnt, acrid, nutty, honey-like.

Hop Aroma/Flavor Descriptors

Floral, perfumy, rose-like, herbal, grassy, tea-like, spicy, peppery, citrus (grapefruit, orange, lemon, tangerine), tropical (mango, passion fruit, guava, papaya), stone fruit (peach, apricot), piney, resinous, dank, catty, onion/garlic (thiol), berry-like.

Yeast/Fermentation Descriptors

Clean, neutral, fruity esters (banana, pear, apple, stone fruit, berry), spicy phenols (clove, pepper, allspice), bubblegum, solvent, rose, honey, bready yeast, sulfury.

Mouthfeel Descriptors

Thin, light, medium, full, heavy, watery, crisp, smooth, creamy, silky, oily, coating, prickly, spritzy, effervescent, warming, hot, astringent, dry, slick.

Balance Descriptors

Even, malt-forward, hop-forward, bitter-dominated, sweet-dominated, dry, well-integrated, disjointed, one-dimensional, complex, layered, harmonious.

Completeness (20 pts)

The Completeness Checklist

Every section of every scoresheet must have substantive comments—at minimum 2–3 sentences for Aroma and Flavor, and at least 1–2 sentences for Appearance, Mouthfeel, and Overall. Blank sections are the fastest way to lose points. Even if you detect nothing remarkable, write something: "Clean, no off-aromas detected. Aroma is somewhat neutral, consistent with style expectations."

Feedback (20 pts)

Feedback can appear in any section but is most concentrated in Overall Impression. Use this pattern:

The Feedback Formula

1. Name the issue → 2. Identify likely cause → 3. Suggest a specific fix

Example: "Noticeable diacetyl (butter) in the finish, likely from incomplete conditioning. Consider adding a 2–3 day diacetyl rest at 65°F before cold crashing to allow the yeast to reabsorb this compound."

Good vs. Bad Feedback Examples

Bad: "Too sweet."

Good: "Residual sweetness is higher than expected for style. Consider mashing at a lower temperature (148°F vs. 154°F) to produce a more fermentable wort, or pitching a more attenuative yeast strain."

Bad: "Needs more hops."

Good: "Hop bitterness is below style range. Consider increasing bittering addition to achieve 40–50 IBU, or adding a late-boil or whirlpool addition for more hop flavor and aroma complexity."

Don't Forget Positive Feedback

Always acknowledge what the brewer did well. "Excellent malt complexity with clean fermentation—very well-made base beer" shows you can recognize quality, not just faults.

Time-Saving Writing Strategies

Exam Day Writing Tips

Abbreviate intelligently: "mod" for moderate, "sl" for slight, "w/" for with. Graders understand common abbreviations.

Use your templates: Memorize the structure for each section so you're not thinking about what to write—just what you perceive.

Don't write novels: Quality over quantity. Three precise, descriptive sentences beat six vague ones.

Write Appearance first while the head is fresh, then move to Aroma while aromas are still volatile.

17-Week Study Plan

Your exam is July 18, 2026. That gives you roughly 17 weeks from today. Here's a structured plan that builds skills progressively. Aim for 3–5 hours per week.

weeks until exam day

Read: BJCP Scoresheet Guide (bjcp.org), this study guide's Scoresheet section cover-to-cover.

Memorize: The five scoresheet sections and point allocations. The scoring ranges (Problematic through Outstanding). Your writing templates for each section.

Practice: Evaluate 2–3 commercial beers per week using full BJCP scoresheets. Don't worry about speed yet—focus on completeness. Compare your notes against the BJCP style guidelines.

Sensory Training: Buy or make an off-flavor spiking kit (available from BJCP or homebrew suppliers). Spike a clean lager with diacetyl, acetaldehyde, DMS, and oxidation. Learn what each smells/tastes like at threshold and above.

Read: BJCP Style Guidelines—focus on the style families most likely to appear (American ales, English ales, German lagers, Belgian ales, wheat beers).

Practice: Evaluate 3–4 beers per week across different style families. Start using the 15-minute timer. Write at least one scoresheet per session under timed conditions.

Drill: Use the flashcards in this guide daily for off-flavor recall. Quiz yourself: "If I detect butter, what do I call it, what caused it, what's the fix?"

Social: Attend a local homebrew club meeting or BJCP study group if available. Tasting with other people calibrates your palate and scoring.

Focus: Speed and feedback quality. Every practice session should be timed at 15 minutes per beer.

Practice: Do at least two full 6-beer mock exams (90 minutes, 6 different beers). Recruit friends to pour for you blind so you don't know the styles in advance.

Feedback drill: After each scoresheet, review your Overall Impression. Does it contain: (1) a style accuracy statement, (2) at least one positive note, (3) at least one specific brewing suggestion? If not, rewrite it.

Calibration: Compare your numeric scores with experienced judges. If you're consistently 5+ points off, recalibrate using the anchor-at-30 method.

Full mock exams: At least one per week. Ask an experienced BJCP judge to grade your scoresheets if possible.

Identify weaknesses: Are you losing points on completeness (leaving sections thin)? Feedback (generic suggestions)? Perception (missing off-flavors)? Scoring accuracy (too generous or harsh)?

Targeted practice: If completeness is weak, force yourself to write 3 sentences minimum per section. If perception is weak, repeat off-flavor spiking exercises. If scoring is off, practice calibration beers.

Expand styles: Make sure you've evaluated beers from at least 10 different BJCP categories.

Final mock exam: Do one last full 90-minute, 6-beer session in exam-like conditions (quiet room, fresh scoresheets, blind beers).

Review: Re-read your templates. Review the off-flavor flashcards one more time. Skim the style guidelines for any categories you haven't covered.

Logistics: Confirm your exam registration. Know the location, start time, and what to bring (pencils—bring extras).

Rest: Don't cram the night before. Stay hydrated. Avoid strongly-flavored foods the morning of the exam. Eat a light, bland meal beforehand.

Exam Day Reminders

Bring: Multiple pencils/pens, water, plain crackers or bread for palate cleansing. Arrive early. During the exam, pace yourself—watch the clock after every second beer. If you're running behind, write shorter but still complete entries for the last beers. A shorter but complete scoresheet beats a half-finished detailed one.
